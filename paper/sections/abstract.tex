\begin{abstract}
Safe RL breaks in a particular way under regime shift: the first symptom is often not a lower mean return, but a controller that becomes jumpy and hard to predict when its own estimates are noisy. This matters in governance-like feedback loops where the operational question is ``what happens on the bad days'' (bursts, attacks, latency spikes), not only what happens on average.

We evaluate a decision-time safety layer that sits on top of PPO: a calibrated uncertainty gate chooses between a learned action and a deterministic fallback, and a deterministic shield enforces hard constraints before execution. The key claim is architectural. The combination (gate + shield) can move the safety--stability operating point under shift relative to policy-only execution, and a compact, classically simulated VQC-style head provides a parameter-efficient uncertainty signal for that routing decision.

Evidence comes from two tiers: (i) standard constrained benchmarks (SafetyPointGoal1-v0 and SafetyHalfCheetahVelocity-v1) with a fixed 30-seed protocol, and (ii) matched 50-seed governance-controller comparisons used as stress tests. To keep the story honest, we also surface a negative result: in one stylized governance ablation, the full system's reward is worse than random. We keep it because it delineates scope: this method targets stable, safe behavior under shift and tight parameter budgets; it is not a guarantee of reward dominance under every reward design.

Repository artifacts and scripts are available upon request.
\end{abstract}
